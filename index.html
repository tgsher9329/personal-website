  • 2019

    JRTC

    Joint Readiness Training Center(JRTC), is located at Fort Polk, Louisiana. It is the Army's premier Combat Training Center. This is a month long training exercise U.S. Army units go to before they are able to deploy to a combat zone. At the time I was in a long reconnaissance, surveillance, and target acquisition (RSTA) unit as a reconnaissance team leader.

  • 2020

    Deployment

    In 2020 I deployed to Nangarhar, Afghanistan. I was apart of a chosen platoon to leave my organic unit, and go with 3/121 of the 48th IBCT. Our mission there was to be uplift for the Special Forces Groups that would be rotating in and out during our stay. We stayed at a forward outstation called "Blackbeard". Most of the time we were there with 10th Special Forces Group. As a whole our responsibilities included keeping the camp operational, its overall security at all times, and many other things. Things that I was either in charge of or at least apart of were various construction projects from barracks to refrigerators all the way to shelving units in our cramped "rooms" and defensive towers for our equipment. I started off the deployment as a 240L machine gunner, got promoted to assistant machine gunner, and eventually was promoted to Staff Sergeant (E6) and was given the opportunity and responsibility of an 11 man squad. In an attempt to not go on forever about my experience over those 9 months, I will leave it at that, feel free to ask me anything, and I will answer it to the best of my ability.

  • 2019

    Border Patrol

    Immediately following the deployment to Afghanistan, I volunteered to go work in texas with the National Guard. I lived in Rio Grande City, and our maun job there was to help the federal agents find, track, and stop narcotics from coming into the country.

  • 2020

    Elite Lineman Training Institute

    While I was in Texas I was trying to figure what path was best for my future. The decision I made was to attend Elite Lineman Training Institute to eventually become a lineman. This school was an amazing experience. It was a challenging experience, because I am not a huge fan of heights, and part fo graduation was climbing a 75 foot tall power pole. I got to learned invaluable skills, got to do something exciting and new everyday, and more importantly met a lot of incredible people that will be life long friends.

  • 2019

    DigitalCrafts

    After starting work as a lineman, I had found an old Python 3 text book that I had and started going through the projects in it. Doing this rekindled my interest in programming. After several months some friends of mine started talking about a tech school to become a full stack developer program and I was immediately interested, but cautious. I had just started a good paying and fulfilling career, but I know I have always been interested in programming. So I took the risk, quit my job, and joined the school to pursue becoming a developer.

Air Assault School

Specialized U.S. Army School

It’s hailed as the toughest 10 days in the Army. Air Assault School takes Soldiers and begins molding them like clay, shaping them into warriors. Only the strongest complete the rigorous training, earning their coveted Air Assault wings.

“It’s the hardest 10 days in the Army and it’s called that for a reason,” Phase 1 Instructor Staff Sgt. John Robertson said. “You have the opportunity to do things in Air Assault School that you don’t get to do every day in the Army.”

The 10-day course is broken into phases, the three different parts of Air Assault they need to know: Phase 1 – Combat Assault, Phase 2 – Slingload Operations and Phase 3 – Rappelling Phase. Before getting to any Phase 1, however, they must first pass Zero Day, where the troops are put through a rigorous obstacle course to see if they have what it takes to begin Air Assault training.

Traditionally, nearly half of each class drops on Zero Day. Day One of the course is focused on the details. The students conducted a layout, where they presented all the equipment they were supposed to bring to the course.

“Throughout the course, they have to be ready to conduct a layout at any point in time, but today is specific in that they’re docked points for any deficiencies they have,” Sgt. 1st Class Sean Olivo, Air Assault course chief, explained. “What this does is teach attention to detail.”

The Air Assault instructors methodically went through all the equipment and then followed up the layout with a grueling “smoking” session and then aircraft orientation.

“They get aircraft orientation, aircraft safety, two pathfinders, a practical exercise on how to set-up an LZ (landing zone), six-mile ruck march and close combat attack,” Robertson explained about Phase 1.

Those who completed the Combat Assault Phase were able to advance to Slingload Operations, where they learn how to properly rig and inspect sling loads. After a full day inside the classroom, they were able to get hands-on training, inspecting the loads.

“We started working on what we call dirty loads, where we put deficiencies within those slingloads,” explained Phase 2 Instructor Sgt. Evan Frank. “Then, we time them, the two-minute standard. When they go through, they’re trying to find all the deficiencies.”

Deficiencies could include anything from debris inside the load, to a spacer being the wrong size. Frank said it’s all about attention to detail. After examining dirty loads, the instructors correct the deficiencies, “so they see what right looks like, so they can tell what wrong looks like during the test,” Frank explained.

Putting everything they’ve learned in Slingload Operations to use, the Soldiers perform a sling load rodeo, where they bind all their rucksacks inside a sling. As a UH-60 Blackhawk hovers about six feet off the ground, they hook the load to the chopper. The Blackhawk goes up about 100 feet and then comes back down to a hover. The Soldiers move in groups of three to quickly perform their task and advance to Phase 3 – Rappelling Phase.

The first thing they do in Phase 3 is learn how to tie a hip rappel seat. The Soldiers start by looping the rope around their waist, between their legs, tuck it through the back and then back around the waist, without deficiencies, in 90 seconds or less.

After expertly learning how to tie a hip rappel seat, they move on to rappelling. The Soldiers then perform one rappel from the wall-side of the tower and then three graded rappels from the open side, one with full ruck and weapon, all from the 100 foot repel tower.

“I have a lot of respect for these guys who have no experience rappelling,” Maj. Jesse Thietten, a dentist with the Fort Hood Dental Activity, said. “The rappel itself with the ruck is a lot of work, but the instructions have been great from the cadre.”

They’ve crawled through mud, ruck marched in the rain, learned information they never thought they’d have to know, survived on little to no sleep and did more overhead claps than they’ve done in their entire lives, all to get to this point – rappelling from a Blackhawk as it hovers 90 feet off the ground.

On the tower, the Soldier on belay had to hold the rope for the Soldier on rappel, but it’s done differently when rappelling from a helicopter.

“Today they’re gonna take off running with the rope, so the rappeller comes down at an angle, as opposed to straight down,” Sgt. Casey Brinkley explained.

“It was exciting to rappel,” Staff Sgt. Leon Reus, Royal Netherlands Air Force, said. Reus is one of three Dutch students who started the course and the only one who completed it.

Day 10 normally consists of a 12-mile ruck march, but because of a predicted storm moving in, they decided to move the ruck march to the night of day nine. Unfortunately for the Soldiers, the bad weather moved into Texas earlier than planned, drenching them in rain as they struggled to complete the course.

On Sept. 11, Class 011-18 began with 117 students. They fought hard, they trained hard, but the rigorous training proved too much for many of them. On Sept. 21, the class graduated 42 students, roughly 35.89 percent pass rate.

Air Assault training is open to all ranks, all military branches and even foreign allies. For those who think they have what it takes to become Air Assault qualified, packets must be submitted and approved by their unit commander.

CIB

Combat Award

The Combat Infantryman Badge (CIB) is a United States Army military decoration. The badge is awarded to infantrymen and Special Forces soldiers in the rank of colonel and below, who have actively participated in direct ground combat.

The next is excerts from the award paper work from the sworn staements provided for the award that has been slightly sanitized.

On 20 April 2019 in the mountains in Sharkay near a certain helicopter landing zone (HLZ). I was with the C2 node and support by fire (SBF). Our element got engaged by by enemy fire. Main effort returned fire with organic weapons and ATG. At around 1630, SBF elements were cleared to engage the buildings. The Carl Gustav team were firing where the enemy fire came from. At the same time, SGT Sheram was cleared by the Ground Force Commander (GFC) to begin engaging with his 240L some targets were ranged to be as far as 1440 meters. I was behind SGT Sheram and team mate fired their weapons in self defense of themselves and friendly forces. During the engagement the teams used organic 120mm mortars, Carl Gustavs, and multiple Air to Ground (ATG) solutions. The enemy combatants were dealt with, and zero friendly casualties were sustained.
